# Federal Right to Record Act of 2026

## What is this?

This is an open-source model federal statute protecting the public’s ability to record federal officers performing official duties in public spaces.

## Why should I care?

Recording public officials helps people preserve evidence, document government action, protect journalists and bystanders, and ensure that public events do not disappear once the moment passes.

## What is the claim?

The core principle is simple: if you have a right to be there, you have a right to record. The model statute protects that right, prohibits retaliation, protects recordings from unlawful seizure or destruction, and creates an independent investigation and civil-remedy structure.

## What is this not?

This is not legal advice, enacted law, or a complete state/local recording-rights framework. It is a federal model focused on federal officers. State and local versions would need to be drafted separately.

## Who is this for?

This model is for lawmakers, advocates, journalists, civil-rights organizations, policy staff, researchers, and members of the public who need a usable starting point for right-to-record legislation.

## How was this done?

The artifact combines a plain-language public overview with model statutory text. It is designed for public use first: readable, shareable, and adaptable without requiring prior familiarity with Structural Flow or Constitutional Physiology.
